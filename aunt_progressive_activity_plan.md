# Progressive Activity Plan

*For an adult in her early 60s: low ejection fraction (26% before cardioversion in late March, current value to be confirmed), atrial fibrillation now treated with ablation and not recurring so far, resting heart rate still low (40s–50s) with the beta-blocker question pending, type 2 diabetes, GERD. She is already active daily — pool, light housework, caring for her cats — and has a home EKG monitor.*

> **How to read this.** This is a conservative, self-limiting progression built on perceived effort, not heart-rate numbers (those don't apply to her right now). It advances **only when she's earned the next step**, and it backs off automatically on bad days. The starting amounts are deliberately low; the intelligence is in the *rules for advancing*, not the exact minutes. Confirm with her cardiologist that progressive exertion is appropriate given her heart rate — that's the one gate at the front. Her home EKG readings are the data that make that conversation productive.

---

## The two gauges this whole plan runs on

**1. The talk test.** She should be able to hold a normal conversation the entire time. If she can't finish a sentence, she's working too hard — ease off. This is the master override; it beats every number below.

**2. Borg perceived-effort scale (6–20).** A simple read of how hard the work *feels*:

| Borg | Feels like | Use it for |
|---|---|---|
| 6–8 | Rest / barely moving | Warm-up, cool-down |
| **9–11** | **Light — could do this for a long time** | **Most of her work, early stages** |
| **12–13** | **Somewhat hard — noticeable but sustainable** | **Ceiling for later stages, only once cleared** |
| 14+ | Hard / very hard | **Not for her right now** |

Her target lives at **11, occasionally 12–13 later on**. Never higher without her doctor specifically okaying it.

---

## How progression actually works (the core rule)

For each stage there are three states:

- **ADVANCE** to the next stage only when she has met *all* the "ready to advance" criteria — typically completing the current stage's sessions comfortably, effort staying at the target level, no symptoms during or after, and quick recovery, sustained across roughly a week (several sessions), **and** her morning resting heart rate and any symptomatic EKG readings have stayed acceptable.
- **HOLD** at the current stage if she's tolerating it but not yet meeting the advance criteria. There is no penalty for staying at a stage as long as needed. Most people hold longer than they expect — that's normal, not failure.
- **DROP BACK** one stage for any day with more dizziness, new or worse swelling, sudden weight gain, more breathlessness, or a worrying EKG reading. Return to the lower stage until she feels solid again, then rebuild.

**One variable at a time, duration before intensity.** When advancing, lengthen *time* first, then *frequency*, and only much later nudge *intensity*. Never increase two at once.

---

## Stage 0 — Establish her true baseline (about the first week)

Before changing anything, find out what she's actually tolerating now.

- **Keep doing exactly what she does** — shallow-water walking/movement, light housework, cats — but start **logging** each session: how long, the talk test (could she speak easily?), Borg effort, any symptoms, and a morning resting EKG plus one taken any time she feels off.
- **Goal of this stage:** a week of data that shows her real current level, and that gives her cardiologist what they need for the clearance and beta-blocker conversations.
- **Advance when:** you have ~a week of readings and (ideally) the doctor has confirmed she's okay to progress and given any rate limits.

---

## Stage 1 — Build a steady base (typically ~2–3 weeks, but hold as long as needed)

**Aerobic (pool):** Shallow or chest-deep water walking — feet on the bottom, head well clear, edge within reach. Broken into short bouts with rest between.

- Start near what she already tolerates — for many deconditioned people that's roughly **3 bouts of 3–5 minutes** with a rest between, working toward **~15–20 minutes of walking total** across the session.
- Effort: **Borg 11 (light).** Full talk test throughout.
- Warm-up and cool-down: 3–5 minutes of easy movement at each end.

**Strength + balance (land), ~2 nonconsecutive days/week:** sit-to-stands from a sturdy chair, heel raises, ankle pumps, gentle arm movements, supported single-leg stands for balance. **One easy set** of each, stopping well short of fatigue. This rebuilds what bed rest stripped away and steadies her on her feet.

**Advance when:** she completes the pool session comfortably, effort stays at 11, no symptoms, she recovers quickly, for about a week.

---

## Stage 2 — Lengthen the time (typically ~3–6 weeks)

**Aerobic (pool):** Stitch the bouts together — fewer/shorter rests — so continuous water walking reaches **~10–15 minutes**, with total session time around **20–30 minutes**.

- Effort: **Borg 11–12.** Talk test still fully intact.

**Strength + balance:** progress to **1–2 sets**, slightly more repetitions or a light resistance band — still genuinely easy, still stopping short of strain.

**Still duration before intensity.** The job of this stage is endurance, not speed.

**Advance when:** the longer session feels light, no symptoms, EKG/log stable, for about a week.

---

## Stage 3 — Gentle intensity and variety (only once stable and cleared)

This is the first stage that touches "somewhat hard," so it's gated hardest: only enter it if the rhythm is still holding, the resting rate is acceptable, and her doctor has okayed adding intensity.

**Aerobic (pool):** Keep most of the session light, but add **brief faster intervals** — walk a little quicker for **1–2 minutes**, then return to easy walking, repeating a few times.

- Effort: easy portions at 11; the faster bits may reach **Borg 13**, never beyond, and never to breathlessness.
- Add variety to keep it sustainable and engaging: sidesteps, gentle arm work through the water, different walking patterns.

**Strength:** modestly increase resistance or reps, keeping effort comfortable.

**Where this is heading:** the general target for someone in her situation is building toward **roughly 20–40 minutes of comfortable activity on most days of the week** — but that's the trajectory if everything keeps going well, not a deadline to chase. Slower is fine. Sustainable beats fast.

**Advance when:** intervals feel manageable, recovery is quick, no symptoms, readings stable.

---

## Stage 4 — Maintenance (the long game)

A sustainable routine she can hold indefinitely:

- **Aerobic:** ~30+ minutes of comfortable water walking/movement most days, effort 11–13, talk test intact.
- **Strength + balance:** ~2 days/week.
- **Variety and enjoyment** built in so it stays something she *wants* to do, not a chore.

**If covered cardiac rehab comes through** (now more likely with the ablation behind her), the best move is to transition into that supervised program — it adds monitoring, education, and check-ins on top of everything here, and a supervised setting is where intensity can safely climb further if her heart allows.

---

## Using the home EKG monitor with this plan

- **One reading each morning at rest** to track her baseline heart rate over time — this is exactly the data the doctor needs for the beta-blocker decision.
- **One reading any time she feels dizzy, fluttery, or off**, ideally during or right after activity. A tracing *while* she's symptomatic is worth far more than a description later.
- **Log the rate and the device's rhythm label** alongside the session notes; export and send tracings to her cardiologist's office.
- **Capture-and-bring, never interpret-and-act.** It's a monitoring tool, not a diagnosis and not a license to change medications. And it does not make solo pool time safe — it records, it can't rescue.

---

## Pool safety (carries over from before — non-negotiable)

- Never in the water alone; phone at the edge at minimum.
- Shallow enough to stand with her head well clear; walking, not treading or deep water.
- Warm, not hot, not cold water.
- **Get out slowly** — sit on the steps a minute before standing, since leaving the water drops the circulatory support and can cause dizziness right at the edge.
- Out of the midday heat; hydrate; not right after a big meal.

## Stop / call / 911

**Stop and rest:** breathless enough to interrupt speech, chest discomfort, palpitations, lightheadedness, nausea.

**Call the doctor same day:** sudden weight gain (~2–3 lb in a day or ~5 lb in a week), increasing swelling, more breathless than usual including needing to prop up to breathe at night, dizziness worse or at rest, persistently very low resting rate with symptoms, a worrying EKG reading.

**Call 911:** chest pain or pressure, fainting or near-fainting, severe shortness of breath, palpitations with feeling faint, bluish lips, confusion. **If she feels dizzy in the water, get to the shallow end / edge and out (slowly) right away — don't wait to see if it passes.**

---

## The two things to confirm with her cardiologist before pushing intensity

1. **Is progressive exertion safe given her heart rate, and is there a rate floor/ceiling to respect?** Bring the home EKG readings.
2. **What is her ejection fraction now?** It may have improved meaningfully once the arrhythmia was controlled — which would widen what's safe — or it may still be low. Either way, knowing it sets how ambitious Stages 3–4 can be.

(The beta-blocker reduction she wants, and any diabetes precautions around exercise — hydration, low blood sugar, foot care near the pool — also belong with her care team, not in a self-directed plan.)
